Memory Games: Reading Comprehension

Text: Memory Games

Most people have trouble remembering where they put their car keys, or if they turned their oven off. And yet an **astonishing few** can memorize entire decks of cards and long strings of random numbers. The documentary Memory Games offers a fascinating glimpse into the lives of these mind athletes as they compete for the title of World Memory Champion. [1] The Post spoke with Nelson Cowan, a cognitive psychologist at the University of Missouri and two competitors profiled in the film – International Grandmaster of Memory Yanjaa Wintersoul, 30, and four-time USA Champion Nathan Dellis, 39 – for tips on how regular folks can improve their daily **recall**.

In high school Yanjaa Wintersoul would never have imagined that she would one day **gain recognition** for having one of the best memories in the world. She explains: “I almost didn’t graduate because I didn’t know how to **make my knowledge stick**. [2] The moment when everything changed for me was several years later when I happened to pick up Joshua Foer’s bestseller on brain techniques. It delves into the science of what makes the human **brain tick** and helps ordinary people strengthen their memories. It was a revelation for me. I began using memorization rules while studying for my business degree in Stockholm and I finished in just two years.”

Nelson Cowan, who does research on how the brain works, says: “It’s not like each memory takes a neuron and then that cell is used up. Imagine you experience a situation or get some information. Your brain first keeps them short-term in a system known as ‘working memory’. [3] Later, if the information stays relevant, our brain encodes it in specific combinations of neurons; the memory becomes long-term.” Our brain can do that an infinite amount of times. From a scientific point of view, the number of facts stored in those patterns is also endless. “If you fill up your 128-gigabyte phone with images, you run out of storage capacity. Our brain doesn’t work like that. [4] The more ‘pictures you take’ and the more you try to remember, the more you will remember,” Cowan adds.

While Yanjaa Wintersoul began competing when barely out of her teens, Nathan Dellis came to the sport a little later in life. [5] “I started listening to people more actively when they were talking about things,” he explains. “Being mindful and paying attention are key to processing the information effectively. There’s plenty of useful daily information worth remembering, which many forget. Every person you meet, try to memorize their name,” advises Dellis. Challenging your brain **on a permanent basis** will likely **yield** amazing results. For instance, he has made a habit of memorizing license plate numbers when stuck in the rush hour. Enough **shut-eye** is also essential. [6] Dellis, for example, attempts to get at least eight hours per night.

When Wintersoul began making a name for herself in the world of competitions, reality shows also began calling. One day, a furniture giant wanted to hire her for an advertisement. They originally asked if she could remember their dining room collection in a week. “I said, ‘I think it would be fun if I learned your entire catalogue by heart.’ And I did, committing 328 pages to memory. Although I can’t recall every **single item** a year later, I do remember a good deal of it, particularly the pages featuring picture-perfect family scenes. [7] Such emotions stick best with me. I think that’s the key to everybody’s memory, you have to make things truly matter to yourself.”

Cowan confirms that memories depend on specific contexts. In a renowned study, participants who learned lists while scuba diving had better recall when they were underwater than when they were on land. Underwater or not – want to stop the did-I-remember-to-turn-off-the-oven paranoia, or at least **get a better handle on** peoples’ names? Keep at it! Even if you aren’t born with **remarkable talent**, with the right strategies, you can become a memory champion too.
